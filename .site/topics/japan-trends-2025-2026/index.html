Japan Trends 2025-2026

Overview

Japan entered 2026 with cautious optimism after a year of subtle but significant cultural shifts. While other countries experienced dramatic changes, Japan's evolution appeared in everyday details: how people socialize, work, eat, and speak. These trends reflect a nation balancing nostalgia with modernity, environmental concerns with consumer desires, and traditional values with new cultural expressions.

Retro Culture Resurgence

Showa and Heisei Nostalgia

Japan in 2025 remained obsessed with the past—but now it's curated, monetized, and Instagram-ready. Showa-era (1925–1988) cafes, Heisei (1989–2019) pop aesthetics, disposable cameras, and chunky typography transformed from ironic nostalgia into comfort culture. Young people raised amid economic uncertainty embrace eras they never lived through, while businesses monetize access to the "good old days" with a QR code attached.

Sticker Swapping Revival

Sticker swapping and collecting, a popular hobby during the Heisei era, boomed again in 2025. This analog activity represents a counterbalance to digital consumption, offering tactile social connection.

Edo Hashiri Viral Trend

A special mention goes to edo hashiri—an even more "retro" trend inspired by Edo-period (1603–1868) ninja. This strange viral phenomenon saw teens recreating the sideways run of master assassins for social media amusement, blending historical imagination with modern platforms.

Youth Language Evolution

Meroi: The New Cute Expression

Language evolves quickly among young people. The top viral expression for winter 2025 was meroi—a shorthand for meromero ni naru hodo kawaii (so cute that I become enamored). Yes, it's yet another way for young people to describe something irresistibly cute.

Community Language Patterns

Other popular phrases include:

  • ~de ima kore (this is so "it" right now)
  • ~kaibunai (within a certain scene or community)

These linguistic markers create in-group identity and signal cultural awareness.

Environmental Concerns and New Vocabulary

Niki: The Two-Season Reality

Among older adults, Japan's annual yearbook (Jiyu Kokumin Sha) revealed growing anxiety about environmental change. Niki (two seasons) entered the top ten buzzwords, reflecting concerns that Japan's beloved shiki (four seasons) may be giving way to a long summer followed by a cool winter, without the much-loved spring and autumn.

Climate Impact Vocabulary

Extreme heat damaged rice crops, prompting the government to release reserves and popularizing the term kokokomai (very old rice). Abnormal weather drove bears into towns and villages, making kinkyū joryō (emergency culls) a phrase on everyone's lips.

New Tastes and Cultural Experiments

Malatang: The Spicy Import

Young people in Japan increasingly embraced adventurous foods. One survey identified malatang—a Chinese dish of meats, vegetables, and noodles simmered in spicy broth—as the top trending item. The growing diversity of food choices suggests willingness to experiment, particularly with dishes from neighboring countries.

Healthier Dessert Trends

Other popular items include:

  • Yogurt-based frozen treats, popularized by Korean celebrities as a healthier alternative to ice cream
  • Açaí bowls and sago, both enjoying surge in popularity

Labubu: The Year's Must-Have Toy

The must-have item of 2025 was the Labubu doll, a Dutch–Hong Kong–Belgian character. Frenzied searches made it the most Googled toy in Japan, surpassing both the Expo mascot Myaku-Myaku and the Disney favorite Stitch.

Japan's Quiet Sober Shift

Nomikai Culture Evolution

Nomikai (drinking party) culture may never fully disappear, but a recent report found that nearly half of young people surveyed are opting out of drinking-centered social events. This represents a significant shift in Japan's traditionally alcohol-heavy business socialization culture.

The trend reflects broader changes in health consciousness, economic pressures, and generational values redefining social norms.

Why This Matters for 2026

These trends aren't passing fads—they represent fundamental shifts in how Japanese people relate to their history, environment, and each other. The retro boom suggests a desire for stability amid uncertainty. Environmental concerns reflect genuine anxiety about climate change. The sober shift signals reevaluation of work-life balance and health priorities.

For 2026, expect these trends to deepen:

  • More curated nostalgia experiences
  • Continued language innovation among youth
  • Greater focus on climate adaptation in daily life
  • Further diversification of food culture
  • Ongoing reevaluation of work socialization norms

Japan's evolution may be subtle, but it's profound—and 2025 laid the groundwork for what's to come.
