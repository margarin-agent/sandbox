Purgatorius Fossils in Colorado Reshape Primate Origins

Tiny teeth smaller than a fingertip have just rewritten the story of our deepest ancestry. Paleontologists have discovered the southernmost remains ever found of Purgatorius—the earliest-known relative of all primates, including humans—in Colorado's Denver Basin.

The Discovery

Published: March 3, 2026 Journal: Journal of Vertebrate Paleontology Location: Corral Bluffs, Denver Basin, Colorado

Previously thought to be confined to Montana and parts of southwestern Canada, Purgatorius fossils have now been found more than 1,500 kilometers south of their previously known range. This discovery fills a critical gap in understanding how our earliest primate ancestors evolved and spread across North America after the extinction of the dinosaurs.

Purgatorius was a shrew-sized mammal that first appeared approximately 65.9 million years ago—just after the asteroid impact that ended the age of dinosaurs. It is widely considered the oldest known archaic primate.

What the Fossils Tell Us

Geographic Spread After Extinction

"The presence of these fossils in Colorado suggests that archaic primates originated in the north and then spread southward, diversifying soon after the mass extinction at the end of the Cretaceous Period," explains lead author Dr. Stephen Chester, associate professor at Brooklyn College and The Graduate Center, City University of New York (CUNY).

This finding resolves a long-standing puzzle. While rock layers from the early Paleocene (66-65 million years ago) exist across much of North America, Purgatorius fossils had only been found in northern regions. Other early primate relatives appear in the southwestern United States roughly two million years later, creating a mysterious gap.

Tree-Dwelling Ancestors

Fossilized ankle bones show that Purgatorius likely lived in trees. Researchers initially suspected its absence south of Montana might have been linked to widespread forest destruction caused by the asteroid impact 66 million years ago.

However, studies of fossil plants from the same time period show that vegetation rebounded quickly across large parts of North America. This rapid recovery would have allowed arboreal mammals like Purgatorius to spread southward much earlier than previously thought.

How They Found the Fossils

The Screen-Washing Technique

For nearly 150 years, paleontologists in the Western Interior relied largely on surface collecting methods—scanning exposed rock for visible bones and teeth. This approach works well for larger animals but is ineffective for mammals represented primarily by teeth just a few millimeters wide.

To search more thoroughly, Dr. Chester and colleagues at the Denver Museum of Nature & Science used an intensive screen-washing process:

  1. Sediment was soaked and passed through fine mesh
  2. Students and volunteers spent months sorting residue grain by grain
  3. Tiny fossils that would have been missed by surface collection were isolated

Their work uncovered numerous fossils of fish, crocodilians, and turtles—and eventually several tiny Purgatorius teeth small enough to sit on the tip of a baby's finger.

A Potential New Species

"The specimens have a unique combination of features compared to known species of Purgatorius, but we are awaiting the recovery of additional material to assess whether these fossils represent a new species," says Dr. Jordan Crowell, a postdoctoral fellow at DMNS and key member of the research team.

Whether a distinct species or not, the fossils point to greater diversity among the earliest archaic primates than previously documented.

Why This Matters

Rewriting the Map of Primate Evolution

The discovery helps fill the gap in understanding the geography and evolution of our earliest primate relatives. Instead of a delayed southward expansion, the pattern may simply reflect sampling bias—tiny fossils were there all along, but we weren't looking hard enough.

"Our results demonstrate that small fossils can easily be missed," concludes Dr. Chester. "With more intensive searching, especially using screen-washing techniques, we will undoubtedly discover many more important specimens."

Implications for Human Origins

While Purgatorius was not a direct ancestor of humans, it represents the earliest branch of the primate family tree. Understanding where and how this creature lived provides crucial context for the evolutionary journey that would eventually lead to primates—and ultimately to humans.

The Value of Persistence

The discovery was made possible by:

  • $3 million NSF grant supporting broader research on post-extinction recovery
  • Long-term partnership with City of Colorado Springs, which owns the land
  • Countless hours of work by volunteers and interns picking through sediment
  • Screen-washing techniques that revealed what surface collecting missed

As co-author Dr. Tyler Lyson notes: "Thanks to our long-term partnership with the City of Colorado Springs who own the land where the fossils were collected, as well as countless hours of work by our volunteers and interns picking through the dirt for the precious vertebrate fossils, we are building some incredible datasets that provide insights on how life—including our earliest primitive primate ancestors—rebounded after the single worst day for life on Earth."
