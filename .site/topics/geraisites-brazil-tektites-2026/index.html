Geraisites — Brazil's First Tektite Field

Scientists discovered Brazil's first known tektite field, "geraisites," evidence of a massive asteroid impact 6.3 million years ago that scattered impact glass across a 900-kilometer stretch of South America.

← Back to home

Overview

For the first time, scientists have identified a tektite field in South America. Named geraisites after the state of Minas Gerais where they were first discovered, these glassy fragments are evidence of a massive asteroid impact that occurred approximately 6.3 million years ago during the late Miocene epoch.

The discovery, published in the journal Geology by a research team led by Álvaro Penteado Crósta (IG-UNICAMP), adds an important chapter to Earth's impact history and fills a significant gap in South America's incomplete record of ancient impact events.

What Are Tektites?

Tektites are natural glasses formed when an asteroid or comet strikes Earth with extreme force. The impact generates temperatures high enough to melt and vaporize rock, which is then ejected into the atmosphere. As this molten material cools while traveling through the air, it solidifies into distinctive glassy shapes before raining back down across vast distances.

Before this discovery, only five major tektite fields were known worldwide:

  • Australasia (Australites) — largest known field, spans thousands of kilometers
  • Central Europe (Moldavites) — used in jewelry since the Middle Ages
  • Ivory Coast (Ivorites)
  • North America (Bediasites)
  • Belize (Belizeites)

Brazil now joins this exclusive club with the sixth major tektite field.

The Geraisites: What They Look Like

Physical Characteristics

Property Details
Color Black and opaque; translucent grayish-green under strong light
Size range Less than 1 gram to 85.4 grams (up to 5 cm in length)
Shapes Spheres, ellipsoids, droplets, disks, dumbbells, twisted aerodynamic forms
Surface Pitted with small cavities (traces of escaping gas bubbles)

The geraisites differ visually from European moldavites, which display a brighter green hue and have been commercially mined for jewelry since medieval times. The Brazilian specimens are darker and more subdued in appearance.

Chemical Composition

Laboratory analysis confirms the geraisites are genuine tektites:

  • Silica (SiO₂): 70.3% to 73.7%
  • Sodium and potassium oxides (Na₂O + K₂O): 5.86% to 8.01% (slightly higher than other tektite regions)
  • Water content: 71 to 107 parts per million — extremely low
    • For comparison: volcanic obsidian contains 700 ppm to 2% water
  • Trace elements: Chromium (10-48 ppm) and nickel (9-63 ppm) — variations suggest non-uniform target rock
  • Rare inclusions: Lechatelierite, a high-temperature glassy silica that forms during extreme heating

The exceptionally low water content is a decisive criterion — tektites are notoriously dry compared to volcanic glasses because the intense impact heat drives off virtually all moisture.

A 900-Kilometer Strewn Field

The geraisites were first documented in three municipalities in northern Minas Gerais — Taiobeiras, Curral de Dentro, and São João do Paraíso — across an area about 90 kilometers long.

After the initial study was submitted, additional finds were reported in:

  • Bahia (northeastern state)
  • Piauí (northern state)

The total known distribution now stretches more than 900 kilometers across three Brazilian states, making it one of the larger tektite fields on Earth.

"This growth in the area of occurrence is entirely consistent with what is observed in other tektite fields around the world. The size of the field depends directly on the energy of the impact, among other factors," explains Crósta.

By July 2025, researchers had collected about 500 pieces. With recent discoveries, that total now exceeds 600.

Dating the Impact

Argon isotope dating (⁴⁰Ar/³⁹Ar) indicates the impact occurred approximately 6.3 million years ago. Three closely grouped age results support this conclusion:

  • 6.78 ± 0.02 million years
  • 6.40 ± 0.02 million years
  • 6.33 ± 0.02 million years

The tightly clustered dates strongly suggest a single catastrophic event rather than multiple impacts.

"The age of 6.3 million years should be interpreted as a maximum age since some of the argon may have been inherited from the ancient rocks targeted by the impact," Crósta notes.

The Missing Crater Mystery

No crater has yet been identified for this impact. This is not unusual — only three of the six major classical tektite fields have confirmed craters:

  • Australasia field: Crater likely lies beneath the ocean
  • Ivory Coast field: Crater unknown
  • North American field: Crater unknown
  • Belize field: Crater unknown

In the case of the geraisites, isotopic geochemistry provides clues about where to look. The analysis indicates the molten material came from Archean continental crust dating between 3.0 and 3.3 billion years old — pointing to the São Francisco craton, one of the oldest and most stable regions of South America's continental crust.

"The isotopic signature indicates a very ancient continental, granitic source rock. This greatly reduces the universe of candidate areas," says Crósta.

Future surveys using magnetic and gravimetric techniques could detect circular underground structures that mark a buried or eroded crater.

Estimating Impact Size

Researchers cannot yet determine the exact size of the object that struck Earth, but all evidence points to a significant impact:

  • Volume of melted rock: Large enough to scatter debris over 900+ kilometers
  • Energy of impact: Sufficient to create a major tektite field
  • Distribution: Wide area consistent with high-energy impacts

The team is developing mathematical models to estimate:

  • Impact energy
  • Entry speed
  • Trajectory angle
  • Total volume of melted material

These calculations will become more refined as additional data on the distribution of geraisites are gathered. The impact was likely powerful, though probably less intense than the event that created the enormous Australasia field (which spans thousands of kilometers).

Significance for South America

The discovery adds an important chapter to South America's impact history. Only about nine large impact structures are currently known on the continent, and most of them are much older than 6.3 million years and located in Brazil.

The findings also suggest that tektites may be more widespread than previously recognized, but are sometimes overlooked or mistaken for ordinary glass. Crósta emphasizes that many people may have encountered tektites without realizing their cosmic origin.

The Research Team

The study involved an international collaboration with researchers from:

  • Brazil (lead institution: IG-UNICAMP)
  • Europe
  • Middle East
  • Australia

Álvaro Penteado Crósta has studied meteorite impact structures since his master's research project in 1978 and has received multiple grants from FAPESP (São Paulo Research Foundation) for this work.

Science Communication and Public Understanding

To address exaggerated claims about asteroid threats, Crósta works with undergraduate students to manage the Instagram account @defesaplanetaria. The page focuses on science communication and aims to distinguish genuine risks from unfounded speculation about meteorites and asteroids.

"Understanding these processes is essential to separating science from speculation," the researcher concludes.

Crósta notes that impacts were common in the early solar system, when debris was abundant and planetary orbits were unstable. Today, the solar system is far more stable, and major impacts are much less frequent — a reassuring perspective for those concerned about cosmic threats.
