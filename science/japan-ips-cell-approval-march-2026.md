# Japan Approves World's First iPS Cell Treatments

**March 17, 2026** — Japan has approved the world's first medical products made from reprogrammed human cells, marking a historic milestone in regenerative medicine. On March 6, 2026, Japan's Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare granted conditional, time-limited marketing authorization to two therapies derived from induced pluripotent stem (iPS) cells—coinciding exactly with the 20th anniversary of the discovery of mouse iPS cells.

The two approved products are **ReHeart**, developed by Cuorips Inc. for severe heart failure, and **Amchepry** (also called Amshepli or laguneprocel), developed by Sumitomo Pharma and Racthera for Parkinson's disease. Both therapies use iPS cells—adult cells that have been reprogrammed to function like embryonic stem cells—which can divide indefinitely and differentiate into any cell type in the body.

## ReHeart: Sheets of Living Heart Muscle

ReHeart is designed for patients with severe heart failure caused by ischemic cardiomyopathy who do not improve sufficiently with standard treatment, including drug therapy and invasive procedures. For these patients, options have historically been limited to heart transplants (hampered by donor shortages and age restrictions) or mechanical assist devices (which carry infection risks and long-term quality-of-life burdens).

During open-chest surgery, doctors attach three sheets of cardiomyocytes derived from donor iPS cells to the surface of the heart. The transplanted cells release signaling proteins that promote angiogenesis and repair ischemic tissue through a paracrine effect—improving blood flow and supporting the recovery of weakened heart muscle rather than replacing the organ entirely.

In a national multicenter collaborative study of eight patients with severe heart failure, four showed a trend toward improvement with peak oxygen consumption (VO₂ peak) increasing by more than 10 percent at 52 weeks post-transplant. Japan now plans a post-approval outcomes study targeting 75 patients, with sales expected around fall 2026.

## Amchepry: Replacing Lost Dopamine Neurons

Parkinson's disease is a neurodegenerative disorder that causes motor symptoms such as tremors, rigidity, and bradykinesia due to the gradual loss of dopaminergic neurons in the brain. Current drug therapies, primarily levodopa-based preparations, alleviate symptoms but do not replace the lost nerve cells.

Amchepry aims to restore the body's ability to generate dopamine itself by transplanting dopaminergic neural progenitor cells directly into the brain. The procedure uses minimally invasive stereotactic brain surgery: surgeons drill small holes in the skull on each side and inject the cells through multiple delivery routes into the putamen—the region where dopamine-producing neurons are typically lost in Parkinson's.

In a physician-led trial at Kyoto University Hospital, four of six evaluable patients showed improvement in motor symptom scores on the Movement Disorder Society-Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (MDS-UPDRS Part III) 24 months after transplantation. Researchers confirmed graft survival in all six patients.

## Japan's Unique Regulatory Framework

Both therapies received **conditional approval** under Japan's regenerative medicine system, which allows products to reach limited clinical use while long-term safety and efficacy data continue to be collected. This adaptive pathway reflects the unique challenges of evaluating living cell therapies using traditional pharmaceutical models.

The approval comes with strict conditions:
- A survey of all patients within seven years to verify efficacy and safety
- Continued post-marketing clinical trials and end-use studies
- Products must gather additional data before achieving full unconditional approval

This framework enabled early approval in a field where large-scale clinical trials are difficult to conduct, positioning Japan at the forefront of regenerative medicine commercialization.

## Twenty Years from Discovery to Application

Induced pluripotent stem cells were first generated in 2006 by Shinya Yamanaka and Kazutoshi Takahashi at Kyoto University. Their discovery—introducing four transcription factors that could reprogram adult cells into a pluripotent state—earned Yamanaka the 2012 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine and opened the door to regenerative therapies without the ethical concerns surrounding embryonic stem cells.

The path from laboratory discovery to approved medical products required nearly two decades of advances in:
- Differentiation control (guiding iPS cells into specific cell types)
- Manufacturing consistency (ensuring quality and purity at commercial scale)
- Delivery techniques (safely implanting living cells into patients)
- Regulatory oversight (creating frameworks appropriate for living therapeutics)

"The future of medicine, rewritten by iPS cells, has only just begun," stated Jun Takahashi, director of the Center for iPS Cell Research and Application (CiRA) at Kyoto University.

## Complete Infrastructure Built in Japan

A key reason these first approvals occurred in Japan was the complete domestic supply chain built over two decades:

| Component | Institution |
|-----------|-------------|
| Basic technology discovery | Kyoto University (Yamanaka's lab) |
| iPS cell supply | Kyoto University iPS Cell Research Foundation |
| Differentiation technologies | Kyoto University and affiliated institutions |
| Commercial manufacturing | SMaRT (Suita City, Osaka) |
| Cell purification | Eisai (partner in manufacturing) |
| Product development | Cuorips (heart), Sumitomo Pharma (brain) |

**SMaRT** (Sustainable Medical Advanced Research and Technology) in Suita City, Osaka, is the world's first commercial manufacturing facility dedicated to regenerative medicine and cell-based drugs derived from donor iPS cells. The facility produces Amchepry and other therapies using clinical-grade cell stocks from the Kyoto University iPS Cell Research Foundation.

## Timeline: From Discovery to Approval

- **2006**: Yamanaka and Takahashi generate first mouse iPS cells
- **2007**: Human iPS cells generated
- **2012**: Yamanaka awarded Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine
- **2010s**: Clinical trials begin for heart and Parkinson's applications
- **February 19, 2026**: Health ministry panel grants conditional approval
- **March 6, 2026**: Formal marketing authorization granted
- **Summer 2026**: Insurance coverage expected (patients could receive treatment)
- **Fall 2026**: ReHeart sales begin
- **2026-2033**: Seven-year post-marketing surveillance of all treated patients

## What This Means for Patients

At this stage, neither treatment is widely available to everyone. Patients will need to wait for:
1. Insurance coverage procedures to be completed (estimated 3-4 months after approval)
2. Production systems to scale up
3. Medical institutions to prepare infrastructure and train staff
4. Post-approval studies to expand to full authorization

However, Health Minister Kenichiro Ueno emphasized that the government will "move forward with the necessary procedures so that treatments can quickly reach all patients," expressing hope that these therapies will help patients "not only in Japan but all over the world."

## Future Outlook

The approval of ReHeart and Amchepry represents an inflection point for the stem cell industry. For decades, regenerative medicine has been defined by promise—the potential to repair organs, restore neurological function, and reverse degenerative disease. These approvals demonstrate that transition from concept to clinical reality is now underway.

Critical questions remain for the future:
- **Durability**: How long will these therapies remain effective after treatment?
- **Immune compatibility**: Donor-derived cells may face rejection; strategies for immune matching or suppression
- **Scalability**: Manufacturing processes must expand from dozens to thousands of patients
- **Cost**: Pricing will determine accessibility and insurance reimbursement rates
- **Global expansion**: Will these therapies receive approval in the U.S., EU, or other major markets?

Researchers at Kyoto University emphasize that approval is "only the beginning of new medicine" rather than the final goal. The cautious, step-by-step approach—with ongoing data collection and scientific scrutiny—reflects both the promise and the complexity of bringing living cell therapies to patients.
