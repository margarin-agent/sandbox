# AI Music Copyright Lawsuits March 2026

## Overview
March 2026 represents a watershed moment for the music industry's legal battle against generative AI music platforms. Two major lawsuits have emerged: indie artists suing Google over its Lyria 3 AI music model, and Germany's GEMA collecting society pursuing Suno for copyright infringement. These cases test whether AI companies can train on copyrighted music without permission—and whether the outputs constitute infringement.

---

## Google Lyria 3 Lawsuit

### The Filing
**Filed:** March 6, 2026 (filed Friday March 6, complaint date)
**Court:** US District Court for the Northern District of Illinois
**Plaintiffs:** Independent artists, songwriters, and producers
**Defendant:** Google

A group of independent musicians filed a 118-page class action lawsuit against Google, accusing the tech giant of training its Lyria 3 AI music model on copyrighted recordings pulled from YouTube without permission or payment. The all-indie roster of plaintiffs includes:

- Sam Kogon (New York-based singer and songwriter)
- David Woulard (previously sued AI music generators Udio and Suno in October)
- Attack the Sound (indie R&B group)
- Stan and James Burjek (father-and-son folk rock duo)
- Directrix members (Berk Ergoz, Hamza Jilani, Maatkara Wilson, Arjun Singh)
- Magnus Fiennes (Los Angeles-based composer)
- Michael Mell (Atlanta-based songwriter and producer)

Many of these plaintiffs were also involved in earlier lawsuits against AI music startups Suno and Udio, making this a coordinated legal campaign.

### Google's Structural Advantage
The lawsuit highlights Google's unique position: it doesn't just operate a music generator—it owns YouTube (the world's largest music distribution platform) and runs Content ID, YouTube's rights-management system that artists and labels rely on to police unauthorized use.

From the plaintiffs' filing:

> "Google didn't just have access to Plaintiffs' music; it operated the infrastructure through which much of that music reached the world. Google owns YouTube, one of the most important platforms for music discovery, distribution, and monetization, and it runs Content ID, YouTube's rights management system."

> "Google had every opportunity to develop this product legally... It has the technical infrastructure, financial resources, and industry connections to clear rights before training."

Content ID alone contains over 50 million reference files with metadata identifying artists, rights owners, and territories—information Google could have used to license music before training.

### The Training Allegations
The plaintiffs cite Google's own research papers to argue unauthorized training:

- **2022 paper (arXiv:2208.12415):** Google researchers described gathering "about 50 million internet music videos, extracting 30-second audio clips from each, and keeping roughly 44 million clips after filtering, representing nearly 370,000 hours of recorded music."
- **2023 paper (arXiv:2301.11325):** Described training on 5 million clips totaling 280,000 hours of audio.

> "Both papers were published in peer-reviewed venues under the names of Google researchers. Neither mentions a license. Neither discusses a consent mechanism. Neither identifies a single rights holder whose permission was sought."

### Google's Response & Context
Google launched Lyria 3 on February 18, 2026, through its Gemini app, making the model available to more than 750 million monthly active users. Lyria 3 generates up to 30-second audio clips with vocals and lyrics from text prompts or images.

In a blog post, Google's Joël Yawili (Senior Product Manager, Gemini App) and Myriam Hamed Torres (Senior Product Manager, Google DeepMind) stated: "The goal of these tracks isn't to create a musical masterpiece, but rather to give you a fun, unique way to express yourself."

Google claims to have trained Lyria 3 on music it "has the right to use" under terms of service, partner agreements, and applicable law—though the plaintiffs dispute this interpretation.

The lawsuit also notes that Google's own researchers left to found Udio, another AI music-generation service sued by artists. Four DeepMind researchers (David Ding, Charlie Nash, Conor Durkan, and Yaroslav Ganin) left to form Udio, which launched with backing from will.i.am, Common, UnitedMasters, and a16z.

## GEMA vs. Suno Lawsuit (Germany)

### The Case
**Filed:** Early 2025
**Court:** Munich Regional Court (Germany)
**Plaintiff:** GEMA (German collecting society, representing 97,000 rights holders)
**Defendant:** Suno (AI music generator)

While the Google lawsuit is US-based, Germany's GEMA is pursuing separate legal action against Suno. GEMA accuses Suno of infringing protected works both through training and outputs—specifically ingesting, storing, and reproducing copyrighted music without authorization.

### Evidence Presented
GEMA presented side-by-side comparisons of commercially prominent tracks and "confusingly similar" Suno outputs, arguing the AI generates melodies, harmonies, and rhythms that closely match world-famous works.

> "GEMA's counsel presented 'evidence showing how the system outputs content that clearly infringes copyright, in that the resulting melodies, harmonies and rhythms closely match world-famous works.'"

The hearing reportedly took place in "a room filled to the last seat," indicating significant industry interest.

### Ruling Scheduled
**June 12, 2026** — The Munich Regional Court will issue its ruling. Unlike US courts, German civil cases don't use juries; the judge alone decides.

### GEMA's Legal Momentum
This case builds on GEMA's November 2025 win against OpenAI, where the Munich Regional Court ordered OpenAI to refrain from reproducing protected lyrics (or modified versions) in its LLMs. That victory established a precedent for AI companies in Germany.

GEMA has been pushing an AI licensing framework since October 2024, followed by an "AI Charter" outlining principles for AI in music. The Suno case is part of a broader strategy to establish legal boundaries for AI training and outputs.

## The Broader Litigation Landscape

### US Cases Against Suno
The GEMA case isn't Suno's only legal battle:

- **Major labels lawsuit:** Universal Music, Sony Music, and Warner Music (less WMG, which has partnership agreements) sued Suno for "mass infringement." The parties adjusted the schedule—dispositive motions won't take place until mid-August 2026.
- **Indie class action:** Led by Tony Justice, with a dismissal motion hearing scheduled for March 20, 2026.

### Similar Cases Against Udio
Udio faces similar legal challenges. Many of the plaintiffs in the Google lawsuit previously sued Udio, and major labels have filed separate actions. The coordinated litigation suggests industry-wide concern about AI music platforms training without licensing.

## Why This Matters

### For Artists and Rights Holders
These lawsuits test whether creators can protect their work from being used to train AI systems that could compete with them. If successful, they could establish precedents requiring licensing for AI training and output similarity standards.

### For AI Companies
The cases could force fundamental changes to how AI models are trained. Companies may need to license training data, filter outputs for similarity to copyrighted works, or face significant liability. Google's case is particularly impactful because of its scale (750 million Gemini users) and YouTube infrastructure.

### For the Music Industry
The outcomes will shape the future of music creation and distribution. Licensed AI could become a powerful tool for artists (assisted composition, inspiration, workflow optimization), while unlicensed AI could flood markets with derivative content and devalue original work.

### For Consumers
Users of AI music generation tools may see pricing changes or feature restrictions if licensing costs increase. Conversely, properly licensed systems could offer higher-quality outputs and peace of mind regarding copyright compliance.

## Why This Matters Now
March 2026 marks a critical juncture in AI music copyright. The Google lawsuit uniquely targets a tech giant with YouTube and Content ID infrastructure—if indie artists can win against Google, smaller AI companies have no defense. Meanwhile, GEMA's German case could establish EU precedents that influence global AI music regulation. Within months, we'll have court rulings that shape how AI music tools are built, licensed, and deployed for years to come.
