# Japan Attractions & Changes 2026

## Overview
Japan's 2026 is shaping up as a transformative year for tourism and culture, with major new attractions opening in Tokyo and Kyoto, significant tax and fare changes taking effect in March, and numerous landmark reopenings after years of renovation. This guide covers everything new travelers and Japan enthusiasts need to know.

## New Attractions Opening in 2026

### PokéPark Kanto (Tokyo)
**Opened:** February 5, 2026
**Location:** Yomiuriland, Tokyo

To mark the 30th anniversary of the Pokémon franchise, the world's first permanent Pokémon park has opened. The 26,000 m² space features multiple immersive zones including the **Pokémon Forest**, which houses over 600 Pokémon statues. Two ticket tiers are available:

- **Trainer's Pass** – Standard access
- **Ace Trainer's Pass** – Premium experience

Both passes include access to Yomiuriland. A separate **Town Pass** for Sedge Town (a city inspired by video games) will launch in summer 2026 for visitors wanting to focus on a single area.

### Museum of Narratives MoN Takanawa (Tokyo)
**Opening:** March 28, 2026
**Location:** Takanawa Gateway City, Tokyo
**Architect:** Kengo Kuma

Designed by renowned architect Kengo Kuma, MoN Takanawa is conceived as an "**experimental museum of culture**" that fuses Japanese tradition with cutting-edge technology to tell untold stories. The museum will feature:

- Twice-yearly thematic exhibitions
- A reinterpretation of "**Inochi no Mirai**" (Future of Life), the signature pavilion from the Osaka World Expo 2025

### Tokyo Dream Park (Tokyo)
**Opening:** March 27, 2026
**Location:** Yaesu, near Tokyo Station

This new cultural hub will house:

- Multiple performance halls
- A rooftop terrace with spectacular city views
- An exhibition space dedicated to Doraemon
- **Rêve des Lumières** – The first Japanese installation by Atelier des Lumières (Paris), featuring large-scale digital art projections for immersive daydreams

### Kawaii Monster Land (Tokyo)
**Opened:** February 13, 2026
**Location:** Basement of Harajuku's Takeshita Street

The long-awaited successor to the Kawaii Monster Café (closed since 2021), this underground theme park celebrates kawaii culture through colorful, interactive zones. Under the artistic direction of Sebastian Masuda, it features:

- Interactive kawaii-themed zones
- Performances by the **Monster Girls**, the franchise's iconic café artists

### Uzumasa Kyoto Village (Kyoto)
**Opening:** March 28, 2026
**Location:** Toei Eigamura Park, Kyoto

A new immersive zone at Toei Kyoto Studio Park, aimed at adults, that recreates the atmosphere of the imperial city during the Edo period. Visitors can immerse themselves in historic Japan through:

- Film sets from historical productions
- Traditional craft workshops
- Hands-on cultural experiences

### Yurakucho Park (Tokyo)
**Opening:** Second half of 2026
**Location:** Yurakucho and Shin-Yurakucho districts

An 18,000 m² cultural and commercial hub integrating art, gastronomy, shopping, and hospitality. In 2027, it will host **JAPA VALLEY TOKYO**, a collaborative space designed by Pharrell Williams and NIGO, inspired by California's Napa Valley.

## Major Reopenings After Renovations

### Edo-Tokyo Museum (Tokyo)
**Reopening:** March 31, 2026

After four years of renovation, this iconic museum dedicated to Tokyo's history since the Edo period (1603-1868) offers a completely redesigned experience:

- New life-size replica of the **Hattori Watch Shop**, a symbol of the Meiji era
- Third-floor plaza redesigned by architect Shohei Shigematsu

### Enryaku-ji Temple (Otsu, near Kyoto)
**Status:** Completes a decade of renovations in 2026

This UNESCO World Heritage Site on Mount Hiei features:

- Main hall (a National Treasure) fully restored
- Many restored paintings
- Breathtaking views of Lake Biwa

### Kodai-ji Temple (Kyoto)
**Status:** Reopening soon after preservation work

Famous for its illuminated autumn gardens, Kodai-ji's renovations include:

- Consolidation of historic buildings (Otama-ya, Kaisan-do)
- Preserved seasonal gardens designed to evoke ephemeral beauty
- Evening illumination visits remain available

### Shuri Castle (Okinawa)
**Partial Reopening:** Autumn 2026

The symbol of the Ryukyu kingdom, devastated by fire in 2019, will partially reopen:

- Main building (**Seiden**) rebuilt identically using period techniques and materials
- Site currently accessible to observe reconstruction work
- Cultural events continue, such as the Shuri Reconstruction Festival

### Hirosaki Castle Keep (Aomori)
**Status:** Returning to original position in 2026

One of the few original Edo-era castle keeps still standing in Japan, it was temporarily relocated for foundation work. **Pro tip:** Visit in spring—the castle is famous for its 2,600 cherry trees, creating a spectacular backdrop during sakura season.

## Tax and Travel Changes Starting March 1, 2026

### Kyoto Lodging Tax Reform
**Effective:** March 1, 2026

Kyoto has significantly revised its tourist tax with a progressive fee schedule based on accommodation luxury level:

| Accommodation Type | New Tax Rate | Previous Rate |
|-------------------|--------------|---------------|
| High-end hotels and ryokan | Up to ¥10,000/night ($64.1) | ¥1,000/night |
| Mid-range accommodations | ¥2,000–5,000/night | N/A |
| Budget accommodation | ~¥1,000/night | Unchanged |

This reform aims to combat overtourism and manage the city's cultural heritage resources.

### Differential Entrance Fees for Foreign Tourists
**Effective:** March 1, 2026

Some cultural sites will charge higher entrance fees for non-resident foreign tourists:

- **Himeji Castle:** Entrance fee doubles for visitors from outside Hyogo Prefecture
- **Note:** Foreign residents with long-stay visas may benefit from local rates with proof of residence

**Recommendation:** Always check official attraction websites before visiting for current pricing.

### Japan Railways Fare Increases
**Effective:** March 2026

JR (Japan Railways) is implementing across-the-board fare increases affecting:

- Local trains
- Shinkansen (bullet trains)

The exact increase amounts vary by route and service type. Travelers should factor this into transportation budgets.

### International Departure Tax Increase
**Effective:** 2026

The international departure tax, set at ¥1,000 since 2019, will **triple** to ¥3,000. This cost is automatically added to airline tickets when leaving Japan.

**Important details:**
- No exemptions for transits
- No exemptions for children (unless announced otherwise)

### Tax-Free System Reform
**Effective:** November 2026

Japan will reform its tax-free system, which currently allows foreign tourists to buy certain products without paying the 10% consumption tax. Expected changes include:

- Higher minimum purchase threshold to qualify
- Restrictions on eligible product categories (possibly excluding certain everyday consumer goods)
- Stricter refund system with enhanced checks at airports and participating stores

**Advice:** Check with local retailers or the Japan National Tourism Organization (JNTO) before making tax-free purchases.

## Closures and Restrictions in 2026

### Hiroshima Castle Keep Dungeon
**Closure:** March 22, 2026 onwards (duration TBD)

The keep will close for major restoration work. However:

- Gardens remain accessible
- Moat and surrounding buildings open
- Hiroshima History Museum on-site

### Nagoya Castle Keep
**Closure:** Until 2028

One of Japan's most imposing castles remains closed for major restoration:

- Exterior can still be admired
- Castle gardens accessible
- Honmaru Palace open
- Temporary exhibitions on-site

### Itsukushima-jinja Shrine Pagoda (Miyajima)
**Closure:** Until December 20, 2026

The five-story pagoda is undergoing renovation:

- Partially hidden by scaffolding
- Main shrine and floating torii remain accessible
- Traditional festivals continue (e.g., Miyajima Water Fireworks Festival)

## Why This Matters
These developments reflect Japan's dual approach to 2026 tourism: investing in world-class cultural attractions while implementing measures to manage overtourism and fund infrastructure. The new attractions showcase Japan's ability to blend tradition with technology (Kengo Kuma's museum, Atelier des Lumières installation), while tax reforms aim to balance economic growth with sustainability. For travelers, these changes mean higher costs but richer experiences—more immersive venues to explore alongside the challenge of navigating new pricing structures. Tokyo's cultural scene continues to rival Kyoto and Osaka, while Kyoto itself expands its contemporary offerings, making 2026 an exciting year to discover Japan's evolving landscape.
