## Overview

Japan's cultural landscape shifted subtly in 2025—not with dramatic gallops but with cautious steps. From retro obsession to sober socializing, young people are redefining work, play, and identity.

## Key Trends

### Retro Revival
**Showa-era nostalgia** is now curated and Instagram-ready. Heisei pop aesthetics, disposable cameras, and sticker collecting have boomed again. Researchers note people flocking to spots with distinct 20th-century aesthetics.

Even stranger: **edo hashiri**—a viral trend where teens recreate Edo-period ninja sideways runs for social media.

### Youth Language
Top viral expression: **meroi** (short for *meromero ni naru hodo kawaii*—"so cute I become enamored").

Other hits:
- `~de ima kore` — "this is so 'it' right now"
- `~kaibunai` — "within a certain scene or community"

### Food Trends
- **Malatang** (Chinese spicy broth dish) — #1 trending food
- Yogurt-based frozen treats (Korean celebrity influence)
- Açaí bowls and sago

### The Must-Have Toy
**Labubu dolls** (Dutch–Hong Kong–Belgian character) became Japan's most Googled toy—surpassing Expo mascot Myaku-Myaku and Disney's Stitch.

### Sober Shift
Nearly **half of young people** are opting out of alcohol at nomikai. Beers replaced by mocktails, specialty teas, or heading home early. Health consciousness, rising prices, and work-life boundaries make "no thanks" socially acceptable.

### Oshikatsu Goes Mainstream
Fandom support (*oshikatsu*) evolved into a **trillion-yen lifestyle**. Fans organize schedules, budgets, and travel around their *oshi*—now openly discussed at work and on dating profiles.

Meanwhile, K-pop's boom is softening: Mrs. GREEN APPLE dominates with young Japanese listeners.

### Climate Concerns
**Niki** (two seasons) entered the top 10 buzzwords—reflecting anxiety that Japan's beloved four seasons may be giving way to long summers and cool winters without spring/autumn.

## Sakura Forecast 2026 (Updated February 26, 2026)

Cherry blossoms expected **earlier than usual** due to warm March/April temperatures. The forecast is based on official observation trees at 58 Japan Meteorological Agency offices.

### Early Bloomers: Kawazu-Zakura

The Kawazu-zakura variety flowers early from late January in warmer areas like Shizuoka's Kawazu town. The Japan Meteorological Corporation now provides forecasts for these early bloomers:

- **Kawazu** (original tree): Around January 22
- **Sasahara Park**: Around February 4

These dates are earlier than in a typical year due to warmer temperatures.

### The Sakura Front

The "cherry blossom front" moves northward:
- **Late March**: Tokyo, Nagoya, Osaka regions begin blooming
- **End of March to Early April**: Hokuriku and Tōhoku regions
- **End of April**: Reaches southern Hokkaidō
- **Late April**: Sapporo blooms (more than 5 weeks after Tokyo)

### Full Bloom Criteria

- **First bloom**: 5-6 flowers have opened on the observation tree
- **Full bloom**: 80% of flowers have opened
- Average time between first bloom and full bloom: ~1 week

## Why It Matters

These trends reveal a Japan redefining connection:
- **Past as comfort** in uncertain times (retro obsession)
- **Health over endurance** in socializing (sober shift)
- **Fandom as identity** when traditional markers feel unstable (oshikatsu mainstream)
- **Climate anxiety** reshaping seasonal culture (earlier sakura, disappearing seasons)
- **Sakura season** remains central to Japanese culture—the symbolic short blooming period connects people through hanami (flower-viewing parties), embodying the philosophy of *mono no aware* (the pathos of things) and celebrating fleeting beauty
