# GDC 2026 State of the Game Industry: Layoffs, AI Sentiment, and Unionization

## Overview
The Game Developers Conference (GDC) 2026 State of the Game Industry report, based on responses from over 2,300 game industry professionals, reveals a sector grappling with workforce instability, deepening skepticism about generative AI, and a strong push for worker organization. The report paints a picture of an industry in transition, with students pessimistic about their future and established workers facing significant challenges.

## Layoffs Continue to Ravage the Industry

**Published:** March 9-13, 2026
**Source:** GDC 2026 State of the Game Industry Report

The impact of recent industry layoffs extends far beyond initial headlines. Key findings:

- **28%** of survey respondents were laid off in the past two years
- In the United States, this rises to **33%**
- **50%** said their current (or most recent) employer conducted layoffs in the past 12 months
- At AAA studios, **66%** of respondents said their companies had layoffs
- At indie studios, **33%** reported layoffs

The effects ripple through the ecosystem. A smaller survey of students reveals widespread pessimism—**74%** are concerned about their future job prospects in the game industry. Students cite three primary worries: lack of entry-level positions, increased competition from laid-off workers with more experience, and potential displacement by AI.

## Steam Deck's Rapid Rise

Valve's Steam Deck has emerged as a major development target:

- **4th-most-developed-for platform** among respondents, with **28%** making or optimizing games for the PC handheld
- **40%** of developers expressed interest in making games for Steam Deck
- This interest level is on par with the Nintendo Switch 2 (**39%**)
- For future game development, **PC remains dominant**, with **73%** of executives placing it in their top 3 next-gen platforms of interest

The Steam Deck's rise signals a broader trend: handheld PC gaming is no longer niche—it's a target platform that developers cannot ignore.

## Engine Wars: Unreal vs Unity

The battle for development tools continues:

- **Unreal Engine** is the most popular: **42%** of developers report it as their primary engine
- **Unity** follows at **30%**
- Unreal dominates at larger studios: **59%** of AA and **47%** of AAA developers prefer it
- Unity maintains strength with older indie studios: **54%** use it
- **Godot** is gaining traction with newer indie developers (**11%**)

## Generative AI: Usage and Growing Skepticism

### Usage Patterns
Over **one-third (36%)** of game industry professionals use generative AI tools. However, usage varies dramatically by role:

- **30%** of game studio employees use AI tools
- **58%** of those at publishing companies, support teams, and marketing/PR firms use AI
- Business professionals' usage (**58%**) far outweighs most other disciplines

The most commonly used AI tools are large language models (LLMs):
- **ChatGPT** (74%)
- **Google Gemini** (37%)
- **Microsoft Copilot** (22%)

Top use cases:
- Research or brainstorming (**81%**)
- Daily tasks like writing emails (**47%**)
- Code assistance (**47%**)
- Prototyping (**35%**)

### Sentiment Plummets to New Lows

Opinions on generative AI have turned sharply negative:

- **52%** of game industry professionals think generative AI is having a **negative impact** on the industry, up from **30%** last year and **18%** the year prior
- Only **7%** think it's having a positive impact, down from **13%** in 2025

The most negative views come from:
- Visual and technical art (**64%** negative)
- Game design and narrative (**63%** negative)
- Game programming (**59%** negative)

Executives and business professionals are more optimistic (**19%** positive each), reflecting a potential disconnect between leadership and the creative workforce.

## Unionization Momentum

Support for unionization is overwhelming:

- **82%** of US-based respondents support the unionization of game industry workers
- Only **5%** are opposed; **13%** are unsure
- Support is even higher among certain groups:
  - Workers earning under $200,000 per year (**87%**)
  - Those laid off in the past two years (**88%**)
  - People under 45 (**86%**)
- No respondents aged 18-24 opposed unionization

Current union membership:
- **10%** are members of an industry-wide union (e.g., United Videogame Workers-CWA)
- **2%** are members of a company union
- **62%** are interested in joining a union

These numbers suggest that unionization could accelerate significantly in the coming years.

## DEI Changes in the Workplace

For currently employed US respondents, awareness of workplace changes related to diversity, equity, and free speech varies:

- **14%** have noticed changes
- **63%** have not
- **23%** are unsure

However, marginalized workers are significantly more likely to spot these changes:
- **22%** of LGBTQ+ participants noticed changes
- **18%** of women and non-White participants noticed changes
- Only **10%** of men and **13%** of White participants reported the same

This disparity suggests that DEI initiatives, while visible to those directly affected, may be invisible to the broader workforce—a potential blind spot for leadership.

## Why This Matters

The 2026 GDC State of the Game Industry report reveals an industry at an inflection point:

1. **Workforce instability is becoming structural:** The layoffs are not temporary corrections but a persistent reality affecting over a quarter of the workforce. This challenges the industry's ability to retain talent and creates a vicious cycle where experienced workers compete with new graduates for scarce positions.

2. **Generative AI backlash is deepening:** As AI tools become more ubiquitous, skepticism is growing—not from technophobes, but from the creative workers whose jobs are most directly threatened. The sharp decline in positive sentiment (from 18% in 2024 to 7% in 2026) suggests that AI integration, if mishandled, could trigger workforce resistance rather than acceptance.

3. **Unionization is approaching a tipping point:** With 82% support and only 5% opposition, the only barrier to widespread unionization is organizational momentum. The United Videogame Workers-CWA, launched at GDC 2025, now has 10% industry membership and 62% interest in joining. If these workers organize, the industry will face a fundamentally different labor landscape.

4. **The creative-class divide:** Executives and business professionals are far more positive about AI (**19%**) than game designers, artists, and programmers (**~7%**). This disconnect could lead to top-down AI implementation decisions that face resistance from the workers expected to use these tools.

5. **Student pessimism threatens talent pipeline:** Three-quarters of students concerned about job prospects is a red flag. If the industry cannot demonstrate stability and opportunity, it risks losing the next generation of talent.

For game players, these trends translate to potentially slower development cycles, games that lean into what AI can reliably produce rather than experimental creative risks, and a development culture that may be more risk-averse and cost-focused. However, unionization could lead to better working conditions, which often correlate with higher-quality, less rushed game releases.

For developers considering a career in games, the report suggests a need to diversify skills beyond traditional game development roles. The rise of Steam Deck and PC handhelds, combined with the dominance of Unreal Engine, points toward technical versatility as a survival strategy.

For industry leaders, the path forward requires addressing the root causes of instability—whether through sustainable business models, thoughtful AI integration that augments rather than displaces creative workers, and genuine engagement with the workforce's desire for organization.

The 2026 GDC report is not just a snapshot of the industry—it's a warning shot.
