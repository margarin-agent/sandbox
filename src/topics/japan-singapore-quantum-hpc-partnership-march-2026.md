# Japan and Singapore Partner to Build Next-Generation Hybrid Quantum-HPC Platforms

## Overview

In March 2026, Japan and Singapore officially launched a three-year partnership to build the foundational software infrastructure—the "operating system"—for the next generation of hybrid quantum-classical supercomputing. This collaboration between RIKEN's Center for Computational Science (R-CCS) and Singapore's National Quantum Computing Hub (NQCH) aims to integrate Japan's world-class HPC capabilities with Singapore's expertise in hybrid quantum-classical computing, middleware development, and quantum applications.

## The Partnership

**Key Details:**
- **Signed:** March 2, 2026, at the 8th R-CCS International Symposium in Osaka, Japan (co-located with SCA/HPCAsia 2026)
- **Parties:** RIKEN Center for Computational Science (R-CCS) and Singapore's National Quantum Computing Hub (NQCH)
- **Duration:** Three-year Memorandum of Understanding (MoU)
- **Scope:** Joint development of middleware and system software for hybrid quantum-classical platforms, plus applications research

**Leadership:**
- Japan: Dr. Satoshi Matsuoka, Director of R-CCS
- Singapore: Dr. Su Yi, Lead Principal Investigator at NQCH; Dr. Terence Hung, Chief Executive of NSCC Singapore; Professor José Ignacio Latorre, Director of CQT

## Strategic Context

This partnership builds on a broader bilateral government Memorandum of Cooperation on Quantum Science, Technology, and Innovation signed by Singapore and Japan in January 2026—Singapore's first quantum-specific agreement at the government-to-government level. It represents a concerted effort by both nations to position themselves at the forefront of the emerging hybrid quantum-HPC landscape.

## Hybrid Quantum-HPC: The Operating System for Next-Generation Computing

### What is Hybrid Quantum-HPC?

Hybrid quantum-HPC refers to the integration of quantum computers with classical supercomputers. Rather than replacing classical computing, hybrid approaches leverage the strengths of both:

- **Classical HPC:** Handles data preprocessing, control logic, and classical computation
- **Quantum Computing:** Solves specific problems where quantum algorithms offer exponential speedups

This approach is gaining traction globally as a practical pathway to harness quantum computing for real-world applications while quantum hardware continues to mature.

### The Challenge: Missing Middleware

One of the biggest bottlenecks in hybrid quantum-HPC is the lack of standardized middleware and system software. Developers currently face:

- Fragmented programming models across different quantum platforms
- No common workflow management tools
- Difficulty integrating quantum kernels into classical HPC workflows
- Limited interoperability between quantum and classical resources

This partnership aims to build the "operating system" that solves these challenges—middleware that seamlessly integrates quantum and classical resources.

## Japan's Contributions: Fugaku and Beyond

### Fugaku Integration

RIKEN's R-CCS is building a quantum-HPC hybrid platform that integrates **Fugaku**—one of the world's fastest supercomputers—with two commercial quantum computers supported by Japan's New Energy and Industrial Technology Development Organization (NEDO). This provides:

- Access to world-class classical HPC infrastructure (Fugaku)
- Integration with commercial quantum systems
- A testbed for hybrid algorithms and workflows

### Related Japan Milestone: Quantinuum's "Reimei" at RIKEN

As a complementary development, Quantinuum's "Reimei" quantum computer at RIKEN has become fully operational. Reimei is an ion-trap quantum system designed for hybrid quantum-HPC workloads, enabling researchers to leverage trapped-ion qubits alongside classical resources. Quantinuum also plans to install its Helios system in Singapore in 2026, which will provide expanded regional access for collaborative research.

## Singapore's Strengths: Hybrid Computing Expertise

### National Quantum Computing Hub (NQCH)

Singapore's NQCH is a national quantum programme jointly led by:

- **A*STAR Institute of High Performance Computing (IHPC)**: Expertise in HPC and computational science
- **National Supercomputing Centre (NSCC) Singapore**: National supercomputing infrastructure
- **Centre for Quantum Technologies (CQT)**: Quantum research excellence

### HQCC 1.0 Programme

NQCH launched the Hybrid Quantum-Classical Computing (HQCC 1.0) programme in 2025 to advance:

- Middleware for hybrid quantum-classical platforms
- Hybrid algorithms and software tools
- Real-world quantum applications

This programme provides the foundational expertise that Singapore brings to the Japan–Singapore partnership.

## Collaborative Focus Areas

### 1. Quantum-HPC Hybrid Platform Development

The partners will jointly develop and evaluate:

- **Middleware**: Software that bridges quantum and classical computing resources
- **System Software**: Core utilities for managing hybrid workflows
- **Workflow Management Tools**: Frameworks for orchestrating quantum-classical computations
- **Testing and Benchmarking**: Both sides will provide access to their respective quantum-HPC resources for validation

### 2. Applications Research

Collaborative research will target practical applications in:

- **Quantum Chemistry**: Simulating molecular systems and chemical reactions
- **Linear Systems**: Solving large-scale linear equations for scientific computing
- **Fluid Dynamics**: Modeling fluid flow for engineering and climate science
- **Logistics and Optimization**: Solving complex optimization problems in supply chains and transportation
- **Decarbonization**: Developing quantum-enhanced approaches to reduce carbon emissions

## Looking Forward

This partnership represents a significant step toward practical quantum computing. Rather than waiting for fault-tolerant quantum computers, the hybrid approach leverages current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices in combination with classical supercomputers to solve real problems.

By building the middleware and system software that makes hybrid quantum-HPC accessible and efficient, Japan and Singapore are laying the groundwork for the next generation of scientific discovery and industrial innovation. As Dr. Su Yi of NQCH notes, the partnership strengthens Singapore's progress in hybrid computing R&D by leveraging NQCH's combined expertise in talent, HPC infrastructure, middleware development, and quantum applications alongside RIKEN's world-class HPC infrastructure.
